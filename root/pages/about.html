My Journey
What actually drives me? - Walk in my shoes

2017 - Turning Point

I discovered coding in the most unexpected way, through the Nickelodeon show Game Shakers. I was fascinated by Kenzie Bell, a 13-year-old programmer who built games for her team. Seeing someone my age writing code and being the technical brain behind everything made programming feel real and within reach. I wanted to do that too. So that year I started learning Java. I did not even have a computer, but that did not stop me. I used my mother’s phone whenever I could, reading tutorials and trying to understand how code worked. I was about the same age as Kenzie, and in my mind that was enough reason to believe I could learn it as well. Around that time I had just taken the National Primary School Examination (NPSE), the cutoff exam students must pass to move on to middle school. I ended up leading my entire school in the exam, something I was incredibly proud of and something that should have marked the beginning of the next chapter of my education. But about a month later, everything changed. Freetown was hit by devastating mudslides, and my family lost our home. We were displaced overnight. Life suddenly became about survival, not plans or achievements. Even though I had done well on the NPSE, I still could not start middle school immediately because of the circumstances my family was facing. That moment began one of the hardest periods of my life and, in many ways, the end of my childhood. I had to step into responsibilities very quickly. I left school for a while because things were too difficult financially. My father’s income was barely enough to keep us from becoming homeless after losing our house. To help the family, I started hawking chocolates on the streets. The small money I made helped with bills at home and sometimes contributed to my younger siblings’ school fees. My days became very simple and very heavy at the same time. I would go out with a tray of chocolates and focus on selling everything before the next day. During that period, it felt like my life had been put on pause. The only thing that mattered was clearing my tray of goods and bringing something back home. The curiosity and excitement I once felt while learning to code had to take a back seat to reality. Still, the interest never really disappeared. Even in the middle of those difficult days, the idea that I had once started learning how to build things with code stayed somewhere in the back of my mind, waiting for the moment when life would give me the chance to return to it.

2018 - Epiphanies

In 2018, I realized I had to get creative. That was the year I discovered entrepreneurship, and it temporarily put my coding journey on pause. If I was going to return to school, I knew it could not happen through hawking chocolates alone. While searching online, I came across the story of a 16-year-old Tanzanian social and environmental entrepreneur named Edgar Edmund. I read how he went to the Anzisha Prize, pitched his venture, and became second runner-up, winning $12,500 to grow his business. I will be honest. At that moment, the money caught my attention. If I wanted to get back to school, I needed resources, and this felt like a path. So I took the little money I had saved from selling chocolates and decided to take a risk. I started a small eco-friendly venture producing charcoal briquettes from waste materials like coconut husks. The idea was simple but meaningful to me. I wanted to tackle the same environmental destruction and deforestation that had contributed to the mudslides that forced me out of school in the first place. My plan was clear in my head. I would grow the business for a few years, then apply to the Anzisha Prize and hopefully become the first Sierra Leonean to win it. Reality hit quickly though. I had just turned 13, and being that young made it very difficult to get clients. People rarely took me seriously. It was hard to convince adults that a child could run a real business. Later that same year, another moment changed my perspective. A Sierra Leonean entrepreneur named Alhaji Siraj Bah, who had also been made homeless by the mudslides, competed in the Anzisha Prize and won first runner-up. When I heard the news, part of me felt like a dream had been taken away. I had imagined being the first Sierra Leonean to reach that stage. But that disappointment ended up becoming one of the best things that could have happened to me. Through that moment, I eventually got to know him. He became a mentor in my life. Since then, I have learned so much from him about entrepreneurship, leadership, and persistence. Eight years later, I am still learning from him. That experience is why I always say that everyone needs a mentor. Sometimes the person who achieves the dream you were chasing ends up becoming the person who helps you build an even bigger one.

2019 - Silver Lining

After running my small business for some time, it had finally grown enough for me to afford going back to school. That year I returned to the classroom, and the feeling was hard to describe. It felt like a major victory. After everything that had happened, simply being able to sit in a classroom again made me feel incredibly accomplished. I was a year behind everyone else. I will not lie, I hated that part of it. It was difficult watching friends move ahead while I had to repeat a year. At the same time, I was deeply grateful that I even had the chance to return. For a moment, it felt like I could be a kid again after almost two years of carrying responsibilities that most children never have to face. But some sparks do not fully return. Those experiences had changed something in me. I found myself constantly thinking years ahead, planning ten steps into the future. I was fourteen and already thinking about where I wanted to be in ten years. That is not really how a teenager is supposed to live. Sometimes I still do not know whether I hate that about myself or appreciate it. That same year I was finally able to get my own smartphone, and I went straight back to coding. I picked up where I had left off and started learning again. Things were moving smoothly. My plan was still in motion. I was turning fifteen the following year, which meant I would finally be eligible to apply to the Anzisha Prize just as I had originally planned. I also applied that year to the Children's Climate Prize. I did not win, but what surprised me most was how I felt about it. I was not disappointed. Instead, I just kept going. The rejection did not slow me down. By that point I had already learned that progress is rarely a straight line, and I had made up my mind that nothing was going to stop me.

2020 - So Much

I decided I was going to take control of the situation I disliked the most. I was still one year behind my peers academically, and I hated that feeling. So I made a decision to sit for the Basic Education Certificate Examination (BECE) a year early. The BECE is the cutoff exam students must pass to enter high school, and preparing for it early meant pushing myself harder than usual. I began studying intensely, expecting the exams to take place in June. Then COVID-19 hit. Lockdowns began, schools closed, my business paused, and suddenly everything felt frozen. For the first time in a while, I had a lot of free time. Earlier that February, I had already applied to the Anzisha Prize. During the lockdown period, I also started teaching myself web development using my mentor’s laptop. With so much time to learn, I went deep into it. By June, I had taught myself enough to confidently build both the front end and back end of web applications. I had effectively become a full stack developer. But academically things were uncertain. The BECE was supposed to happen in June, and I had been preparing for months, yet the exam never came because of the pandemic. I was devastated. All that preparation felt like it had nowhere to go. Eventually the exams were announced again, this time scheduled three months later. By that time, I had been topping my classes consistently since returning to middle school. Some of my classmates knew this, and others had known me since primary school. A few of them reached out asking if I could help tutor them for the upcoming exam. I agreed immediately. Since my family was going to be away for a while, I invited a group of them to stay at my house for the three months leading up to the exams. We basically turned my home into a study camp. Every day we studied together for hours. I explained concepts, helped them work through problems, and we pushed each other to improve. Teaching them helped me understand things even better myself. Looking back, it was a perfect example of the Feynman Technique in action. About a month into our study camp, I received an email saying I had advanced to the quarter-finals of the Anzisha Prize. I was incredibly excited, but we still had a bigger task ahead of us. The exams were approaching, and that had to remain the focus. In September, we finally sat for the BECE. Then something happened that I will never forget. About an hour before my final paper, I received another email from the Anzisha Prize. This time it said that I had not been selected to continue. The timing could not have been worse. My mind was racing and I felt crushed, but there was nothing I could do in that moment except walk into the exam hall and finish what I had started. A few months later, in December, the results were released. Every single person who had stayed with me during those three months of studying scored within the 99th percentile. After all the work they had put in, it felt completely deserved. As for me, I was honored to finish first in the entire country. After such an unpredictable and emotional year, it felt like the best possible ending to a very eventful chapter of my life.

2021 - Wins

After such an eventful 2020, I entered 2021 with a very clear mindset. I sat down at the beginning of the year and mapped out where I wanted to be by the following year. I knew I was going to apply again to the Anzisha Prize, but this time I wanted a backup plan. That was when I discovered the African Leadership Academy. It is widely considered one of the most prestigious schools on the continent for young leaders. My plan was simple. I would apply to both and work hard enough that at least one opportunity worked out. That year brought a lot of unexpected milestones. I became a global finalist in the inaugural year of the Rise. I was also featured in Diaspora Focus Network magazine for my academic achievements the previous year, which is considered one of Sierra Leone’s most respected publications. I even appeared on national television on Life by Design, hosted by Joe Abass Bangura. At the same time, I had just started high school. For a moment, it felt like everything in my life was finally aligning the way I had imagined during those difficult years. I also spent the better part of that year learning C# and game development in Unity, I made simple and effective 2D games that I would play on my mobile phone in isolation and comfort. But I got excited a little too quickly. The first decision I received that year was from the Anzisha Prize, and it felt like a repeat of the previous year. Once again, I did not make it past the quarterfinal stage. It was disappointing, but by then I had learned how to move forward. I told myself that maybe the other plan would work out. Not long after, the decision from the African Leadership Academy arrived. This time the news was different. I had been admitted. In that moment, it felt like a new chapter of my life had begun. The years of setbacks, persistence, and long-term planning were finally opening the door to something bigger. This year was also the end of an era, getting into ALA meant that I would be moving to South Africa, my business was not as big to run without me and I couldn't stay back just for it. My mentor made me an offer for it, I needed the money to sponsor my 2 years at ALA so I logically had no reason to reject, I must sy though, unitl this day, it is not a decison that i regret even in the slightest. I had put so much work into it and it felt nice to have some payout, and besides the whole point of starting it was to get back into school, if selling it meant I was going to a school that presented opportunities tenfold, it was a decision that I was happy to make.

2022 - To be(long) or Not to be(long)

2022 was largely a year of preparation. Everything revolved around getting ready to leave for the African Leadership Academy. The year began with a long and exhausting process of gathering the documents needed for my South African residency permit. I spent weeks moving from one hospital to another trying to secure the medical certificates required for the application. At the same time, I was juggling school, travel paperwork, and the growing reality that I would soon be moving across the continent. As news spread that I had been admitted to the African Leadership Academy, things at school became difficult. Instead of support, the announcement created resentment. Some peers and even a few teachers reacted with jealousy. I experienced hostility that I had not expected. There were moments when I was treated unfairly, and the environment became emotionally draining. During that period, I had one safe space: Edmund Davies. Mr. Davies had been part of my life long before this moment. I first met him in 2017, during the time when I had stopped attending school and was hawking goods on the street. One day he recognized me and invited me to attend private lessons at his home free of charge. It was an act of kindness I will never forget. Over the years he became far more than a tutor. He was a guardian, a teacher, and a friend. In 2022, when things at school began to deteriorate, I confided in him often. I spoke to him about losing friendships, about the unfair treatment I was experiencing, and about the things people said to me. One comment from a teacher stayed with me more than anything else. I was told that I would never amount to anything because my parents were uneducated. According to that teacher, even if I became educated, it would not change my future. Those words still echo in my mind today. While I was navigating all of that, another fear was growing quietly in the background. I had applied for my South African residency permit twelve weeks earlier. I had originally been told the process would take about eight weeks, but there was still no response. Every passing day made me more anxious. It began to feel like everything I had worked toward might collapse at the last moment. Then, exactly four weeks later, I finally received the call. The permit was approved. The process had taken twice as long as expected, but it was done. I was going to the African Leadership Academy. A new chapter of my life was beginning. At seventeen years old, I was leaving home and moving to a country where I did not know a single person. In September of that year, I landed in Johannesburg. My first thought when I stepped off the plane was simple: What have I done? Until that moment I had not really felt afraid, but suddenly the reality of everything hit me. I was still just a kid, and I had no idea what the next chapter would hold. What I did not realize at the time was that this was only the beginning of a deeper struggle. The imposter syndrome slowly crept in. By December, my confidence had taken a serious hit. I was surrounded by students who had accomplished incredible things. Everyone seemed extraordinary, and in comparison everything I had done suddenly felt small. For the first time in a long while, I began to question whether I truly belonged there. And that feeling quietly set the stage for what 2023 was about to bring.

2023 - When life knocks you down

2023 was the year I began to truly adapt to life at the African Leadership Academy. After the doubts and imposter syndrome that followed my arrival, this was the year things slowly started to come together again. One of the first full-circle moments happened early in the year when I became a web development intern at the Anzisha Prize in South Africa. For years, I had dreamed about being part of that organization as a fellow. Instead, I arrived there in a completely different capacity, working behind the scenes as a developer. And honestly, I did not hate it at all. I had a lot of fun. During the internship, I worked on improving and maintaining parts of the organization’s web platforms. I helped build and refine front-end interfaces, contributed to backend functionality, fixed bugs across different parts of the site, and helped ensure that pages were responsive and accessible. I also supported updates to program pages and internal tools that helped showcase the work of young African entrepreneurs. Being able to contribute technically to an organization I had admired for so long felt incredibly rewarding. But that was just the beginning of my growth that year. 2023 quickly became the year I expanded my horizons the most. I traveled more in that single year than I had traveled in my entire life before. I also secured another internship, this time in consulting at the African Leadership Consulting Group, where I worked with teams to analyze problems and develop strategic recommendations. That same year I also became a semifinalist in the Harvard–PwC Case Competition, an experience that pushed me to think critically about business strategy and problem-solving on a global stage. Toward the end of my first year at the African Leadership Academy, I was selected to become a peer counselor. In that role, I helped support younger students transitioning into the academy, offering guidance, listening to their concerns, and helping them adjust to the demanding academic and social environment. It was about creating a support system so new students would feel less alone during their transition, something I deeply understood after my own first months there. Outside of academics and leadership roles, I also began exploring new parts of myself. I picked up photography and started developing a creative outlet through visual storytelling. I even joined the marketing department of Riyadha Sports Agency, where I helped with branding and content. I also developed a strong interest in international relations. I became involved with the African Leadership Academy Model African Union as an intern and participated in major conferences across the continent. I attended YALE Model African Union 2023 in Rwanda and ALA Model African Union 2023 in South Africa as a delegate. In the summer of that year I spent three months in Dakar, Senegal as a BUILD-In-A-Box camp facilitator, I was part of a team tasked with inspiring youths and teaching them about entrepreneurship to have them start their own journey, another full circle moment for me. I had become to other people what Alhaji Siraj and Mr.Davies were to me. By the end of the year, something had shifted again. The confidence that had been shaken when I first arrived at ALA was coming back. Slowly but surely, I was beginning to feel like I belonged.

2024 - Next!

In 2024, the growth I had been working toward for years started to become very visible. I was only a few months away from graduating from the African Leadership Academy, and many of the roles I had taken on during my time there were beginning to evolve. One of those moments came when I was promoted to committee chairperson at the ALA Model African Union. Leading a committee meant guiding delegates through complex discussions about the future of the continent, moderating debate, and helping shape thoughtful resolutions. By the end of the conference, the research guide I had written for the committee was published for future delegates to use as a literature review and preparation document. At the same time, I had fully fallen in love with my role as a peer counselor at the African Leadership Academy. That role meant a lot to me. I spent time supporting new students as they adjusted to life at the academy, listening to their experiences, and helping them navigate the pressures of a place that can feel overwhelming when you first arrive. Being able to offer that kind of support felt meaningful, especially because I had once been the student trying to find my own footing there. By March, the ALA Model African Union conference was underway, and I was enjoying every moment of leading my committee. The discussions were thoughtful and intense. We debated the future of African policy, development, and cooperation while learning from peers who were just as passionate about the continent as I was. At the same time, another major question was hanging over my head. I had to figure out what would come after ALA. That meant applying to universities around the world. The biggest challenge was financial. I had sold my business years earlier to help fund my journey to ALA, and there was very little money left for university. Despite that uncertainty, I applied to twelve universities. When the results came back, I had been accepted into eight: Texas Christian University, Constructor University, Morehouse College, American University of Beirut, University of Connecticut, Pomona College, Syracuse University, and University of Richmond. In the end, I chose the American University of Beirut for two main reasons. First, it was the strongest financial option available to me. Second, the upcoming United States elections created uncertainty about possible policy changes that might affect international students. I have always had the habit of thinking far ahead, and that uncertainty made studying in the U.S. feel like a risk I was not ready to take. So on the day I turned nineteen, I officially enrolled at the American University of Beirut on a full scholarship. Two months later, I graduated and became an alumnus of the African Leadership Academy. That summer brought even more full-circle moments. I secured an internship at Wanda World, where I gained professional experience working in a creative environment focused on storytelling for young audiences. It was a meaningful opportunity that helped tie together many of the skills and lessons I had developed during my time at ALA. Later that same summer, I returned to the Build in a Box program, this time as a project manager during my second year with the initiative. The difference was that I was now stationed in my home country, Freetown. Coming back home in that role felt powerful. It was another full-circle moment. The child who had once been forced to leave school and sell chocolates on those same streets was returning with new experiences, new knowledge, and a completely different sense of purpose. The person who left was not the same person who had come back.

2025 - Questions

At the end of 2024, in September, I moved to Lebanon to start university—a new country, a new city, and a completely new chapter. But barely a month into the semester, the situation changed dramatically. Israel attacked Lebanon, and the conflict escalated quickly. I began questioning whether I had made the right choice. I have to say, I was privileged. As a Mastercard Foundation Scholar, the foundation prioritized my safety and the safety of every other scholar. Even though the semester had just begun and circumstances were chaotic, they ensured we could continue our studies safely. I moved back home, took classes online, and was flown to nearby Ghana to sit for exams, courtesy of the foundation. At the beginning of 2025, a ceasefire was declared, and I was able to return to Lebanon and engage with the university in person. Having lived through transitions and strange places before, I quickly adapted. I built a community, developed a reliable workflow, and gradually everything began to fall into place. I took on leadership roles almost immediately. I became a chairperson at the Beirut Model United Nations UN conference in Lebanon"], where I applied the knowledge and experience I had gained from ALA Model African Union. I joined the 180 Degrees Consulting Club, became an active member of the Computer Science Student Society, and also served on the council of the African Club. Volunteering remained a priority for me. Even after the chaos of the war, I made sure to dedicate at least five hours every week to giving back. That summer, I secured an internship as a software engineer at Africell Group, Sierra Leone’s premier telecom provider. I also returned to Build in a Box for the third time, taking on the role of project manager for the second time in Freetown. That period felt like everything I had worked for over the past decade was converging. The experiences, skills, and resilience I had built over the years were finally finding a space to grow and make an impact.
